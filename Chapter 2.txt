The envoy’s face remained a cavern of shadow beneath his hood, but his presence was heavy, a gravitational pull that made the oxygen in the station feel thin.
Jinwah didn't move. His hands were shaking—not from the cold, but from the phantom sensation of the market stalls dissolving under his touch. The "sweetness" he’d spent twenty years cultivating felt like a shattered porcelain mask.
"Answers?" Jinwah echoed, his voice cracking. "The only answer I want is how to stop it. How to turn it off."
The envoy tilted his head. It was a bird-like, predatory movement. "You speak of your gift as if it were a disease. The Lights see a key. We see a legacy. You cannot 'turn off' the Void, Jinwah. It is not a lamp. It is the ocean, and you are currently drowning because you refuse to swim."
"I killed people today," Jinwah whispered, looking at the grime on the station floor. "I didn't mean to. I just... I didn't want them to touch me."
"The price of divinity is often paid in mortal coin," the envoy said coldly. He stepped out of the shadows, revealing robes that seemed woven from smoke and obsidian. "Come. The Disciples are not fools. Aurora Starblade is already weaving a net of light across this sector. If you stay here, you will become a specimen in a golden cage."
Jinwah looked back toward the distant glow of Shelter-7. He thought of Elara and her whirring glass eye. He thought of the life he’d built out of scrap and quiet kindness. It was gone. The "Light" had burned it to ash the moment they spoke his name.
He stood up, his legs feeling like lead. "Where are we going?"
"To the Grey Zones," the envoy replied, turning toward the rusted monorail tracks that disappeared into the fog. "To the throat of the world."
The journey was a blur of muted colors and mechanical groans. They traveled via a "Shadow-Skiff"—a low-profile transport that hummed with a frequency so low it made Jinwah’s teeth ache. It stayed within the deep canyons of the industrial waste, avoiding the sweeping sky-beams of the Council’s surveillance drones.
High above, Jinwah saw the glittering spires of the High Cities, where the Lights lived in eternal noon. Down here, the world was an endless twilight of rust and acidic mist.
"Why me?" Jinwah asked after an hour of silence. "There have been other hybrids. They all die. Why did I survive the strain?"
The envoy didn't look back from the controls. "Hybrids die because they are accidents. Biological glitches. You, however, were an investment."
Jinwah’s blood ran cold. "An investment by who?"
"The Empress does not speak to investments through intermediaries," the envoy said. "But ask yourself: why would a half-human heart beat in time with the Void? Why would the entropy that dissolves all things find a home in your marrow? You weren't born, Jinwah. You were negotiated."
Before Jinwah could press further, the Skiff banked sharply. The ground beneath them seemed to open up—not a hole, but a massive, jagged fissure that glowed with a faint, violet luminescence.
This was the entrance to The Ossuary, the subterranean stronghold of the DEÆ.TH-allied faction. As they descended, the scale of the place became apparent. It wasn't a cave; it was an architecture of bone. Massive ribs of some prehistoric, divine beast formed the arches of the tunnels, reinforced by black steel and pulsing conduits of dark energy.
The Skiff docked at a platform crawling with figures. These weren't the "pure" warriors of the Light. They were grim, scarred, and diverse. He saw Voidans with skin like cracked basalt and humans with heavy cybernetic augmentations.
The moment Jinwah stepped off the Skiff, the air changed. The "Void strain" in his chest, usually a sharp, stinging needle, suddenly felt... quiet. Like a tuning fork finding its match.
"Wait here," the envoy commanded.
Jinwah stood alone on the platform, his human form feeling fragile in the face of so much darkness. Then, a shadow detached itself from the ceiling above him.
It dropped silently, landing with the grace of a cat. It was a woman, her body wrapped in shifting, translucent fabrics that blurred her silhouette. Her skin was pale, almost translucent, and her eyes were a sharp, piercing emerald.
"So," she said, circling him with a predatory curiosity. Her voice was like velvet over gravel. "This is the miracle boy. The one who can make more of us."
Jinwah recoiled. "I'm not a miracle. And I'm not a breeder."
She laughed, a short, sharp sound. "Relax, kid. I'm Vesper Blackwood. I’m the one who has to keep you alive while the Empress decides if you’re a weapon or a messiah."
She stopped in front of him, her gaze scanning the "unremarkable" face he still wore.
"You look like a tech-slug from the shelters," she noted, reaching out to flick a piece of dust off his shoulder. "But I saw the footage from Shelter-7. You’ve got a lot of ghosts inside you, Jinwah. Try not to let them out until I tell you to."
"I don't want to hurt anyone else," Jinwah said firmly.
Vesper’s expression softened, just a fraction. It wasn't kindness—it was recognition. "In this world, sweetheart, 'not wanting to hurt anyone' is the fastest way to get everyone you love killed. Come on. The Empress is waiting in the Inner Circle. She’s been dreaming about you for twenty years. It’d be rude to keep a goddess waiting."
She turned and began walking into the heart of the bone-city. Jinwah looked back at the Skiff, then at his hands. He took a breath, adjusted his hood, and followed her into the dark.
