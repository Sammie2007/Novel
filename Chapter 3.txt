If the shelters were a lie told in neon and pixels, the Ossuary was a truth written in calcium and shadow.
As Jinwah followed Vesper Blackwood deeper into the subterranean throat of the world, the air changed. It lost the artificial ozone crispness of Shelter-7’s recycled atmosphere. Here, it tasted of ancient dust, cold minerals, and a faint, metallic tang that Jinwah realized, with a shiver, was the scent of dried blood and old magic.
He felt small. Not just because of the scale of the caverns—which were vast enough to house entire skyscraper-tier cities—but because of the weight of the history pressed into the walls.
"Don't stop," Vesper’s voice drifted back to him, flickering like a stuttering video feed as her light-bending robes interacted with the violet glow of the conduits. "The floor is more sentient than it looks."
Jinwah looked down. The ground beneath his boots wasn't stone. It was a mosaic of polished, dark ivory. Thousands of fragments of bone, fused together by obsidian grout, formed a path that hummed beneath his feet. He felt a sickening lurch in his stomach—the "sweet and humane" part of him recoiled at the macabre architecture—but his "other" half, the part that had roared to life in the market, felt a terrifying sense of recognition.
He saw the inhabitants of the dark.
Groups of Voidans stood in the hollowed-out alcoves of the rib-arches. Some were massive, their skin like cooling lava, cracked and glowing with internal heat. Others were spindly, their limbs elongated into graceful, deadly points. They didn't talk; they seemed to communicate in low-frequency vibrations that Jinwah felt in his marrow rather than heard in his ears.
There were humans, too. But they weren't like the cowering vendors of the market. These were soldiers of the shadow—men and women with limbs replaced by matte-black hydraulics, their eyes swapped for glowing violet lenses that tracked his movement with predatory hunger.
I am a prize to them, Jinwah realized, his heart hammering against his ribs. A breeding stock. A weapon. An investment.
The thought made him feel cold, an icy dread that pooled in his gut. He was twenty years old, and he’d spent his life trying to be "good." He’d fixed broken machines, shared his rations with Elara, and tried to forget the name Voidheart. Now, he was walking through a graveyard built for living gods, being led toward a woman who had "dreamed of him" before he was even born.
Then, they reached the Great Rib-Gate.
It was the skeleton of a titan, its massive spine forming a bridge across a lake of liquid shadow. As they crossed, Jinwah looked over the edge. He didn't see water. He saw the Void—a churning, infinite blackness that seemed to swallow the light from Vesper’s robes.
At that moment, the "Void strain" in his chest—the sharp, stinging pain he’d lived with since childhood—did something it had never done before.
It opened.
The branching dark circuits on his arms didn't manifest, but he felt the energy stir beneath his skin. It wasn't a tear anymore; it was a sigh. For the first time in his life, the pressure inside him matched the pressure outside him.
The silence was gone. In its place was the Bone-Chorale—a million whispers echoing from the walls, the floor, the very air. They weren't speaking words, but intentions. Hunger. Grief. Ancient, cold fury.
"It's quieted down, hasn't it?" Vesper asked, stopping at the threshold of the Inner Circle. She didn't turn around, but her silhouette seemed sharper against the violet gloom.
"How did you know?" Jinwah gasped, clutching his chest.
"Because the first time I came here, I thought I was going deaf," she said, finally turning. Her emerald eyes were wide, reflecting the liquid shadow below. "The Void doesn't hate you, Jinwah. It just wants to be reunited with you. That's what you're feeling. Homeostasis."
"I don't want this to be home," he said, his voice a desperate whisper. "I want to go back to the rain. Even the fake rain."
Vesper stepped closer, her shifting robes brushing against his human sleeve. For a second, her face was clear—pale, scarred, and weary.
"There is no 'back,' kid. Aurora Starblade made sure of that. You've escaped the Light, but you haven't escaped the war. You’re just finally seeing the other side of the blade."
She gestured toward the massive, obsidian doors at the end of the bridge. They were carved with the likeness of a figure staring into a sun that was being eaten by a serpent.
"Behind those doors is the Empress," Vesper said, her voice dropping to a low, respectful rasp. "She is the childhood friend of DÈÆ.TH. She is the prophecy-keeper. She is going to look at you and see every version of the future that ends in blood."
Jinwah looked at the doors. He felt like a lamb being led into the den of a wolf, but a lamb that had accidentally swallowed a star.
"What do I do?" he asked.
"You survive," Vesper said, the light-bending fabric of her cloak beginning to flicker again as she stepped toward the door. "And for gods' sake, Jinwah... try to stay 'humane' for as long as you can. It’s the only thing that’s going to make her curious enough not to kill you immediately."
The obsidian doors began to grind open, and a wave of absolute, terrifying silence spilled out. Jinwah took a breath, felt the nebula stirring in his blood, and stepped into the presence of Death.
