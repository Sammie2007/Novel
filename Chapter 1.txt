
In the beginning, there were gods. But the gods broke.
Some held to their purity, becoming the cold, radiant Lights. Others fell, consumed by the dark energy of the Void... they became the Voidans.
One name predates the fracture, whispered in both fear and awe: DÈÆ.TH. The first to embrace the Void. The first to turn from the light. To the Voidans, he is not a god. He is the god.
Their war tore reality apart. Humanity was left to survive in the ashes, hiding in shelters of steel and scrap, living under skies where their gods still fight.
From the chaos, an impossibility was born. A child of both worlds. Half Voidan. Half human. A hybrid who should not exist.
His name is Jinwah Voidheart.
Some see a key. Others, a catastrophe. But no one knows which side he will choose... Or if he will choose his own.
Chapter One
The rain over Shelter-7 wasn’t real. It fell in perfect intervals, a digital simulation piped through cracked domes, programmed to remind mortals what storms used to feel like. Still, it made the under-market smell of ozone and damp metal, the scent of life pretending not to be synthetic.
Holographic carp swam through the air above the stalls, their translucent scales flickering between advertisement scripts for recycled tech and vat-grown noodles. Beneath that shifting light, Jinwah Voidheart kept his head down, hood drawn low, another ghost moving through the press of bodies.
He’d taken his favorite disguise—unremarkable. Slender, dark-haired, quiet-eyed, the kind of face that dissolved from memory the moment someone looked away. He stopped at a stall layered in old fiber cables and salvaged data chips. The woman behind it, Elara, had one organic eye and one glass one that whirred softly as it refocused.
“Just the converter I reserved,” Jinwah said, voice calm but tired.
Elara smiled, all crow’s-feet and solder burns. “You and your machines. Always fixing things no one cares to keep running.” She slid a corroded power converter across the counter. “Clean pull. Tested it myself.”
He traded her a few scratched credit chits and gave a small, genuine smile. “You’re a lifesaver.”
For a moment the guarded anonymity fell away, replaced by a quiet warmth. It was his small rebellion—the choice to be kind in a world built on control. He wasn’t fighting wars or gods tonight. He was just being human.
Then the market went silent.
Not quiet—muted. A sudden pressure drop, the simulation itself faltering. The rain froze mid-fall, suspended pixels trembling in the air before vanishing. Holograms winked out. Neon signs died.
A single shaft of light—cold, pure, merciless—cut down through the shelter’s ceiling.
Jinwah felt it before he saw it. The chill that crawled along his nerves, hunting the part of him that wasn’t supposed to exist. The Lights.
Crowds collapsed to their knees, half in worship, half in terror. Jinwah stayed standing. That small defiance felt louder than a scream.
Three figures descended through the radiant breach, walking where mortals dared not look. Their armor gleamed white and gold, each step burning away grime that had clung for decades. At their head strode a woman with hair like molten fire and eyes of weaponized grace.
Aurora Starblade. A Disciple of RESURRECTION. A zealot.
“Jinwah Voidheart,” she said, her voice melodic steel. “Your vigil is over. The Light calls you home.”
He kept his hands visible, palms up. “Wrong person. I’m a technician.”
Aurora’s lips curved into a pitying smile. “We know what you are—the last hybrid of the old blood. A flaw in the divine plan that has become our most precious asset.”
The title hit harder than any blade. Around them, the crowd stared, the name Voidheart whispering through the silence like contagion.
“Asset?” he echoed.
“You can reproduce.”
The word hung, sterile and cruel.
“The Lights cannot. You are not a man. You are a key.”
Her two companions drew their weapons, humming with sanctified power. Jinwah’s heartbeat slammed in his chest. For a heartbeat, he almost surrendered—until the memory of being hunted since birth burned through the fear.
“No,” he whispered.
Aurora inclined her head. “That was not a request.”
The Disciples moved. Too fast. Too perfect.
His “no” became a roar.
The human mask shattered.
It wasn’t flesh that broke—it was reality bending. His skin split like glass under pressure, revealing a storm beneath: swirling dark nebula, circuits of shadow threading across his limbs. His eyes imploded into black holes that reflected nothing. The Void strain—the curse that should have killed him—rose like a crown.
The nearest Disciple froze mid-step, awe curdling into fear.
Jinwah slammed a palm against the ground.
The world unmade itself.
No explosion, no fire—just absence. Light folded in, stalls collapsing silently as their molecules forgot how to exist. The Disciples were hurled backward, armor cracking, divine glow flickering like broken code. Aurora threw up a shield of holy radiance; it warped under the pressure, her composure finally cracking into something human—terror.
He saw her face through the rippling dark, and for an instant hated what he saw reflected there. Monster.
The thought gutted him.
Then the noise returned—screams, alarms, collapsing steel. Jinwah stumbled back, horror slicing through the haze. Elara crouched behind her counter, her human eye wide with disbelief.
He ran.
Through smoke and dying neon, through ruptured circuits and fleeing bodies. The digital rain rebooted above him, falling again in stuttering bursts as if the world itself was confused.
By the time he burst from Shelter-7 into the open industrial wastelands, real rain was falling—acidic, heavy, unfiltered. His lungs burned. He didn’t stop running until the lights of the city blurred behind him.
At the edge of the territories stood a decaying monorail station, its pylons gnawed by rust and time. He collapsed inside, every breath shaking. The Void energy receded, leaving him hollowed and raw.
“She was right,” said a voice.
Jinwah jerked upright. A figure stood in the far shadows, robes black and motionless despite the wind. No reflection in the puddles. No signature in his senses.
“Who are you?” Jinwah rasped.
The hooded envoy stepped forward, voice calm, deliberate. “She said you would come to this place tonight. We have been waiting.”
“‘She?’”
“The one who saw you when the gods did not.”
A pause, then words that felt like prophecy.
“The Empress of Death offers you sanctuary, Jinwah Voidheart. And answers.”
The storm outside thickened, drowning the world in gray.
He looked at his trembling hands—human again, yet stained by something infinite—and realized the war had already found him.
End of Chapter One
