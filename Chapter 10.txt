Back in the smog-choked tunnels of Shelter-7, the market was a graveyard of broken dreams and scorched metal. The "Holy Light" of the Disciples had faded, leaving behind only the dim, flickering neon of a world that was trying to reboot itself.
Elara sat on a crate in the middle of her ruined stall. Her glass eye whirred in a jagged, broken rhythm, unable to focus on the piles of melted data chips and shattered power converters. But her mind was focused on something else. She was thinking about the boy.
She remembered the day he "spawned." That was what the people in the shelter called it when an orphan just appeared out of nowhere. Jinwah had been six years old, standing in the middle of the market with grease on his nose and a broken flashlight in his hand. He didn't know his parents. He didn't know his descent. He just knew that Shelter-7 was where the air was warm and the people were kind.
"He was just a boy," Elara whispered to the empty air.
She remembered watching him grow up. He was the "cute little technician" of the sector. While other kids were playing in the waste-vats, Jinwah was sitting at her counter, his small hands moving with a speed that shouldn't have been possible. He found joy in the hum of a working engine. He found peace in making things "right" again. He was the sweetest soul she had ever known—always sharing his credit chits, always fixing Elara’s whirring eye for free.
She had loved him like a son. She thought she knew everything about him.
But when the Lights came, the world she knew had shattered.
She had been right there. She had seen Aurora Starblade call him by that name: Voidheart. She had seen the way his skin cracked like glass. She had seen the "un-making" wave that turned her neighbor’s stall into dust.
It wasn't just the power that scared her. It was the face. For a second, the boy she had raised was gone. In his place was a creature of the Void—a being with black-hole eyes and a crown of dark, swirling stars. He looked like a god of death, standing in the middle of her home.
Is that who he always was? she wondered, her hands trembling. Was the sweetness just a mask? Was the little technician just a lie he told to hide the monster?
She remembered the look on Jinwah’s face right before he ran. He had looked at her with his cosmic eyes, and for a split second, she saw the boy again. He looked terrified. He looked like he wanted to apologize for existing.
The other survivors in the shelter were screaming for his blood. They called him a demon. They cursed his name and thanked the Lights for "saving" them from the hybrid in their midst. They questioned how they could have let a monster live among them for twenty years without noticing.
Elara couldn't join them. But she couldn't defend him, either.
How do you defend someone who can erase reality with a touch? How do you love a boy who is the very thing that broke the world?
She stood up slowly, her joints aching. She looked at the small power converter Jinwah had bought from her just before the fight. It was sitting in the dirt, untouched. She picked it up, feeling the cold metal against her palm.
She couldn't decide. Her heart wanted to go find him, to tell him it was okay, to give him a hug and tell him to come home. But her mind kept replaying the image of his skin splitting open and the darkness spilling out.
She didn't stay to help the others clean up the market. She didn't talk to the Council investigators. She just turned away from the ruins of her life and walked into the shadows of the lower levels. She left the situation behind, unable to choose between the son she loved and the god she feared.
Jinwah Voidheart was a ghost now—a memory of a kind boy that had been swallowed by the dark. And Elara, the woman who knew him best, was the only one who realized that the most dangerous thing about him wasn't his power.
It was his heart.
